Tuberculosis
(TB)

"The world's deadliest infectious disease – curable with DOTS."

Epidemiology & Overview

Tuberculosis (TB) – highly infectious chronic disease caused by Mycobacterium tuberculosis. Primarily respiratory but can affect other organs. In 1993, WHO declared TB a global emergency.

Philippines Situation

  • Ranks 9th among 22 high‑burden countries (WHO watchlist).
  • Estimated incidence: 243/100,000 population/year (2006 report).
  • TB ranks 6th leading cause of morbidity and mortality (2002).
  • National TB Control Program (NTP) target: Cure ≥85% of smear‑positive cases; detect ≥70% of estimated new cases.

Global Burden

TB remains a leading killer worldwide. Multidrug‑resistant TB (MDR‑TB) and HIV co‑infection worsen the burden. The Philippines is committed to MDG Goal 6: Combat HIV/AIDS, malaria and other diseases (target: halt and reverse TB incidence).

Transmission & Pathogenesis

Mode of Transmission

  • Airborne droplet nuclei – coughing, sneezing, singing, talking.
  • Infectious dose: small; close contact with smear‑positive patients.
  • Bovine TB: ingestion of unpasteurized milk (rare in PH).
  • Period of communicability: as long as viable bacilli in sputum; effective chemotherapy reduces infectivity within days to weeks.

Susceptibility & Risk Factors

  • Highest risk: Children <3 years, adolescents, elderly, immunocompromised (HIV).
  • Underweight, undernourished, diabetics, silicosis, substance abusers.
  • Primary infection: most remain latent; reactivation years later.

Tap/Hover

5 Elements of DOTS

1. Political commitment
2. Quality-assured sputum microscopy
3. Standardized short‑course chemotherapy (supervised)
4. Uninterrupted drug supply
5. Recording & reporting system

Clinical & Diagnosis

Signs & Symptoms (TB Symptomatic)

  • Cough ≥2 weeks (most important)
  • Fever, night sweats
  • Chest/back pain
  • Hemoptysis or blood‑streaked sputum
  • Significant weight loss, fatigue, malaise

Diagnosis

  • Direct Sputum Smear Microscopy (DSSM) – primary diagnostic tool.
  • Three sputum specimens (spot, morning, spot).
  • Chest X‑ray (supportive, not definitive).
  • TB Diagnostic Committee (TBDC) for smear‑negative cases.
  • Culture & DST for MDR‑TB suspects.

🔬 Case definition:

Smear‑positive PTB – at least 2 initial sputum smears positive OR 1 positive plus CXR consistent with TB. Smear‑negative requires TBDC evaluation.

H = Isoniazid, R = Rifampicin, Z = Pyrazinamide, E = Ethambutol, S = Streptomycin. Fixed‑dose combinations (FDCs) preferred.

Directly Observed Therapy (DOT): No patient should start treatment without a treatment partner (health worker, BHW, family member). DOT ensures adherence and prevents resistance.

Prevention & BCG

  • BCG vaccination – given to all infants at birth or soon after (EPI). Protective efficacy ~50% against any TB, 64% against TB meningitis, 71% against death from TB.
  • Prompt diagnosis and treatment of infectious cases (source control).
  • Improve ventilation, reduce overcrowding.
  • Health education on cough etiquette, sputum disposal.
  • Isoniazid preventive therapy (IPT) for child contacts <5 years old (with negative chest X‑ray) where resources available.

🏥 NTP Vision: "A country where TB is no longer a public health problem."

PHN Role & Nursing Care

  • Case finding: identify TB symptomatics (cough ≥2 weeks) and refer for sputum microscopy.
  • Assign and supervise a treatment partner for DOT.
  • Maintain and update TB Register (Form 1, 2, 3).
  • Facilitate drug requisition and distribution; ensure no stock‑outs.
  • Health education: adherence, sputum collection, cough etiquette, nutrition.
  • Childhood TB: perform tuberculin testing if trained, refer for CXR, assist in child contact management.
  • Coordinate with Barangay Health Workers for defaulter tracing and home visits.
  • Submit quarterly reports (FHSIS) to Provincial/City Health Office.
Linkages: PHN coordinates with TB Diagnostic Committee, Rural Health Midwives, hospitals (for sputum microscopy), and local government for political support.
